Introduction

What is Golf AI?

A mobile-app startup based in San Diego, CA using 3D pose-estimation technology to analyze and improve golf swings.

As 1 of 2 visual designers, I was responsible for the visual identity of the company. For the app, we received midfis from the UX team and developed hifis to handoff to the developers. I also used video production and motion design to create both in-app and promotional content.

11 hi-fi app flows, design system

Brand Major Project 1 of 2

Challenge Point 1

How can we further develop the company’s brand?

Buzzwords

Both the UX team and users desired the app to be a soothing and encouraging coaching experience with a balance of fun/credibility.

This translates to demonstrative/engaging visuals and helpful copy, without the stress of information overload.

Colors

Using process of elimination on 4 color-word association charts, our winning colors with recurring characteristics were: green for growth and orange for confidence.

The specific shades were chosen with respect to a contrast-checker.

Our monochrome palette for text (primary, secondary, tertiary) referenced Adobe.

Typography

We continued to use the apps’s existing font, Lato. For our newly-decided 8 pt grid system, we determined line-heights (ranged 120%-150%) for box sizes to be multiples of 4 in height.

We also wrote use cases for developer reference.

This was a continuously developing process as the number of screens grew.

Building Components Major Project 2 of 2

Foundation

The first step was to build standard components. For ideas on what we needed, I referenced the Golf AI app at that point, as well as Airbnb’s design system. Ex: Navigation icons, form elements.

We added app-specific components as the number of screens grew, so we could efficiently fill in content! Ex: Drill headers, action plan cards.

Overall Visual Style

To create the desired “soothing” and lightweight experience, we limited boldness. We mainly used 2px strokes for actionability and active states, while using light gray box shadows to group and separate elements.

Learning Points

Scaling Down

Our initial hifis were in iPhone X size, based on the midfis we received. After noticing our first handoff had errors, I pushed for us to start smaller (esp. if everything fit on one page). From that point on, all of our main references were for iPhone 8.

Vertical Responsiveness (Login Screen)

I had difficulty calculating margin sizes (in orange) so all device versions looked balanced. A developer suggested a more flexible approach: group content to dynamically center. This saved me time from fixating on numbers, and I started to rely more on Figma’s smart constraints.

Validating Designs (User Tests)

Before user tests, we gave our UX Researcher interactive prototypes with generated questions. Our common questions were often visual focused, some being how do users...

  • understand what iconography choices represent
  • see how elements look interactive based on boldness/size/color
  • interpret their golf skill progress or statistics based on colors
  • decide what to do (based on the structure/hierarchy of the screen)
  • feel about the mood

Note: Over Zoom (due to COVID-19), our UXR controlled the mouse for Figma prototypes and we relied on verbal answers. We had to keep in mind not having accurate accounts of scrolling and tapping.

Information Architecture (Premium Screen)

Our biggest challenge screen involved finding a balance between 3 pieces of information onto the Premium screen (pricing, benefits, and app purchase actions).

Price Model

  • A limited time offer of “3.99 per month for 3 months”
  • Monthly price → $8.99/month after the 3 months
  • Annual price → $39.99/year

  • 7 benefits taken from the previous Premium page

    • App Purchase

  • Privacy Policy
  • Restore Purchases

    • Note: We (the designers) misunderstood the model to be $3.99 total for 3 months” for our initial iterations. The annual pricing also changed from $59.99 to $39.99 during our process.

    ITERATION 1: 2 columns to save vertical space

    For early iterations, we divided the monthly/annual choices into 2 columns. With limited width, we found it difficult to make the price standout.

    We unnecessarily repeated text (monthly, annual, $3.99…)

    Our CTA choice was an unconventional height with poor color contrast

    ITERATION 2: CTA focused on price

    Using our standard CTA allowed us to focus on the price. However, we needed a better way to directly reflect the pricing models under the limited time offer.

    It might take more effort to breakdown annual savings at a glance.

    The buttons styles (1 line vs. 2 lines) are inconsistent. The per-month pricing doesn't match an annual concept.

    Final Iteration: CTA with sub-info

    We decided on a button style already in our design system!

    Monthly: $3.99 / month was chosen as the primary text so the user could see what they're paying upfront. The subinfo explains pricing after the limited time offer ends.

    Annual: We used the second line so the user could see how much they are saving compared to the monthly price.

    Development Handoff

    Problem point 3

    How can we improve designer-development handoff?

    According to the previous UX team, difficulties communicating resulted in inconsistent spacing. My HTML/CSS background helped me frame how we might share language and what to give the developers moving forward.

    We had pre-development meetings for devs to validate the structural logic of our hifis. For any questions about feasibility, we tried to save effort and address them before finishing mockups. By our third week of handoffs, we were consistently presenting 3 main aspects.

    Zeplin

    Pseudocode

    Previously, handoff for Zeplin didn’t include descriptions. I started to write CSS-based notes on element behavior (finding the best margin, positioning, or sizing rules for all devices).

    Example cases:

    • Elements positioned relative to the bottom or right
    • Using proportional height for devices (ex: this image should be 50% screen height)
    • Fixed sizes (ex: this card has a fixed height of 80pt) v. responsive sizes (ex: the banner should be 100% screen width)

    Improving Organization

    We began numbering screens (ex: Drills 01, Drills 02) and used tags for easier grouping/sorting.

    Version History

    I added commit messages to update if any components/screens changed.

    Figma

    Interaction Annotations

    Being able to see all artboards at once, we created annotations for microinteractions and how screens connected.

    This is also where we placed iPhone X and 6/7/8+ appearances and prototypes for the devs to reference.

    Google Drive

    We created folders for each screen flow and placed our assets there.

    Different Hats Motion graphics, marketing, etc.

    Working in a start-up environment I was able to practice self-delegation and flexibility. Whenever our hi-fi work was stalled, I had opportunities to promote the app in other ways: video production and motion design.

    Areas to Grow

    Working practically

    One of my biggest personal/professional challenges was remembering to choose practicality over perfection (and pushing pixels)! I had to work through my discomfort asking for feedback when a design wasn’t fully fleshed out. Going forward, my goal is to get comfortable sharing my early stages so I can focus on high-level understanding of a flow.

    This applies to working cross-functionally too. Our process for handing of development specs was trial-and-error that never reached 100% perfection. I realize that the 3 developers we worked with each have their own coding/organizational styles, and as a designer it’s better to be flexible than exact in order to work together.
